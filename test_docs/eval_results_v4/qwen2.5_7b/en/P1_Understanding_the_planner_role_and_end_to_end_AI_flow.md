## Understanding the Planner's Role within the Platform

Before creating scenarios or initiating calculations, you need to understand where planning fits into the platform and what is expected of your role.

As a planner, you work on the part of the process that prepares future operations. Your job does not consist in resolving real-time incidents nor managing tactical execution for today’s day-to-day activities. Instead, it involves preparing a coherent base plan that other modules and teams can use later.

Use this quick start when starting as a planner for the first time, to clarify responsibilities between planning and operation, or to understand what your work should deliver to the rest of the platform.

Before you begin:
1. Ensure you have access to the environment with the **Planner** role or equivalent visibility.
2. Have a basic understanding that the platform is divided into several functional areas.
3. Be willing to first comprehend the flow before setting up data or calculating scenarios.

To understand planning's role within the platform:
1. Open the platform and identify key areas related to planning, rostering, and daily operations.
2. Review the overall workflow and understand that planning occurs prior to day-to-day execution.
3. Think through this process in order:
   1. **Planning** prepares future scenarios and builds a coherent base.
   2. **Rostering or Roster Planning** connects these plans with real people, labor rules, and availability.
   3. **Daily Operations** manages what actually happens during today’s day-to-day activities.
4. Confirm that your role as a planner belongs to the planning layer rather than real-time operational management.
5. Use this workflow rule as a reference: if you are preparing future plans, you are likely working in the correct place as a planner.

When you finish this section, you should understand that planning feeds subsequent modules and that your work occurs before tactical execution.

## Differentiating Planning from Operations and Vehicles

After understanding where planning sits within the platform, the next step is to comprehend what falls under your purview and what does not.

As a planner, you are responsible for preparing the logic of planning and ensuring that the scenario is sufficiently coherent for its subsequent use. This means that your work will include tasks such as creating scenarios, validating the scheduling base, and preparing outputs that can later be consumed by Scheduling, Rostering, and Operations.

Before proceeding, keep in mind this idea:
1. Visibility does not equate to responsibility.
2. Although you may have a broad view of the system, you should not intervene across all domains.
3. Your role must remain focused on future planning, not dispatch or live incident resolution.

To distinguish your role from that of other profiles:

1. Review what you need to do as a planner:
   1. Prepare scheduling scenarios.
   2. Build a coherent base for Scheduling.
   3. Verify if the plan logic makes sense.
   4. Deliver outputs that can be used by subsequent modules.

2. Review what **does not** belong to your role:
   1. Resolve same-day incidents in real-time.
   2. Reassign drivers to cover immediate issues.
   3. Act as though you are responsible for live dispatching.

Compare your role with **Operations**:

1. Planning prepares what should occur.
2. Operations manage what is currently happening now.

Compare your role with **Vehicles**:

1. Planning works on vehicle type logic and future viability.
2. Vehicles work on the physical fleet, maintenance, and current availability.

Use this decision rule when you have doubts:
1. If you are designing or adjusting the future plan, it belongs to planning.
2. If you are resolving a live operational issue in real-time, it belongs to operations.
3. If you are updating the physical state or fleet availability, it belongs to vehicles.

When you finish this section, you should be able to explain the difference between planning, operations, and vehicles without mixing responsibilities.

## Confirming What Subsequent Teams Receive from Planning

Your work as a planner does not end when you create a scenario. It ends when you prepare an output that other teams and modules can actually use.

This is important because weak planning bases generate downstream issues. Rostering may struggle to organize the workload, operations might be forced to correct more things in real-time, and fleet-related decisions become harder to interpret.

Before moving on to the next quick start, make sure you understand that outputs from planning should be:
1. Clear.
2. Coherent.
3. Usable.
4. Traceable.

To confirm what the rest of the platform receives from planning:
1. Review the scenario or planning output you are preparing.
2. Ask yourself if another team could understand and use it without having to guess your intentions.
3. Confirm that you are not creating something just for yourself, but a foundation that other modules will consume later.
4. Check that you are not mixing future planning decisions with today's operational actions.
5. Use this final rule as a reference:
   1. If you are preparing the future plan, it is in the domain of planning.
   2. If you are resolving an issue from today, it is already in the operations domain.
   3. If you are correcting physical fleet availability, it is in the vehicles domain.

When you finish this section, you should be ready to start building the planning foundation in the next quick start.
