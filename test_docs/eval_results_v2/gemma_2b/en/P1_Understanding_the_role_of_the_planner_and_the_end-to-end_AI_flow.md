## Understanding the Planner's Role within the Platform

Before creating scenarios or initiating calculations, you need to understand where planning fits within the platform and what is expected of your role.

As a planner, you work on the preparatory stage of the future operation. Your work does not involve resolving real-time incidents or managing the tactical execution of the current day. Your work consists of preparing a planning base that other modules and other teams can use afterward.

Use this quick start guide when you are first starting as a planner, when you need to clarify responsibilities between planning and operations, or when you want to understand what your work should deliver to the rest of the platform.

Before you begin, make sure that:
1. You have access to the environment with the **Planner** role or equivalent visibility.
2. You have a basic understanding of the platform being divided into various functional areas.
3. You are willing to understand the flow first before configuring data or calculating scenarios.

To understand the planner's role within the platform:
1. Open the platform and identify the main areas related to planning, lists or rostering, and daily operations.
2. Review the overall flow and understand that planning occurs before the daily execution.
3. Think about the process in this order:
   1. **Planning** prepares the future scenario and builds a coherent base.
   2. **Lists or rostering** connects this plan with real people, labor laws, and availability.
   3. **Daily operations** manages what actually occurs during the current day.
4. Confirm that your role as a planner belongs to the planning layer and not real-time operational management.
5. Use this work rule as a reference: if you are preparing the future plan, you are likely working in the correct place as a planner.

When you finish this section, you should understand that planning feeds subsequent modules, and your work occurs before tactical execution.

## Differentiating Planning of Operations and Vehicles

After understanding the location of planning within the platform, the next step is to understand what is your responsibility and what is not.

As a planner, you are responsible for preparing the planning logic and ensuring that the scenario is sufficiently coherent for your use later. This means your work will include tasks such as creating scenarios, validating the planning base, and preparing outputs that can be consumed by Scheduling, Rostering, and Operations.

Before continuing, keep in mind this idea:
1. Visibility is not the same as responsibility.
2. Although you may see a broad part of the system, you should not intervene in all domains.
3. Your role should remain focused on future planning, not on dispatching or correcting live incidents.

To distinguish your role from the work of other profiles:
1. Review what you must do as a planner.
   1. Prepare planning scenarios.
   2. Build a coherent base for Scheduling.
   3. Review if the plan's logic makes sense.
   4. Deliver outputs that other modules can use later.
2. Review what does not belong to your role.
   1. Resolve incidents of the same day in real-time.
   2. Reassign drivers to cover immediate problems.
   3. Act as if you were responsible for live dispatching.
3. Compare your role with **Operations**.
   1. Planning prepares what should occur.
   2. Operations manages what is currently happening.
4. Compare your role with **Vehicles**.
   1. Planning works with the logic of vehicle types and future viability.
   2. Vehicles works with the physical fleet, maintenance, and current availability.
5. Use this decision rule when you have doubts:
   1. If you are drawing or adjusting the future plan, it belongs to planning.
   2. If you are resolving an operational problem in real-time, it belongs to operations.
   3. If you are updating the physical state or availability of the fleet, it belongs to vehicles.

When you finish this section, you should be able to explain the difference between planning, operations, and vehicles without mixing responsibilities.

## Confirming what subsequent teams receive from planning

Your work as a planner does not end when you create a scenario. It ends when you prepare a output that other teams and other modules can actually use.

This is important because a weak planning base generates problems downstream. Rostering may have difficulty organizing work, operations may be forced to correct more things in real-time, and decisions related to the fleet may become more difficult to interpret.

Before moving on to the next quick start, be sure to understand that planning outputs must be:
1. Clear.
2. Coherent.
3. Usable.
4. Traceable.

To confirm what the rest of the platform receives from planning:
1. Review the scenario or planning output you are preparing.
2. Ask yourself if another team could understand and use it without having to guess what you meant.
3. Confirm that you are not creating something just for yourself, but a base that other modules will consume later.
4. Verify that you are not mixing future planning decisions with operational actions of the same day.
5. Use this final rule as a reference:
   1. If you are preparing the future plan, you are in the planning domain.
   2. If you are resolving an incident today, you are in the operations domain.
   3. If you are correcting the physical availability of the fleet, you are in the vehicles domain.

When you finish this section, you should be ready to begin building the planning base for the next quick start.
