## Understanding the Planner's Role within the Platform

Before creating scenarios or initiating calculations, you need to understand where planning fits within the platform and what is expected of your role.

As a planner, you work on the part of the process that prepares future operations. Your work does not consist of resolving real-time incidents or managing the tactical execution of today's day. Your work consists of preparing a planning base that other modules and other teams can use later.

Use this quick start when you are starting as a planner for the first time, when you need to clarify responsibilities between planning and operations, or when you want to understand what your work should deliver to the rest of the platform.

Before you begin, make sure:
1. You have access to the environment with the **Planner** role or equivalent visibility.
2. You have a basic idea that the platform is divided into several functional areas.
3. You are willing to understand the flow first before setting up data or calculating scenarios.

To understand the role of planning within the platform:
1. Open the platform and identify the main areas related to planning, rostering, and daily operations.
2. Review the overall flow and understand that planning occurs before daily execution.
3. Think about the process in this order:
   1. **Planning** prepares the future scenario and builds a coherent base.
   2. **Rostering** connects this plan with real people, labor rules, and availability.
   3. **Daily Operations** manages what actually happens during today's day.
4. Confirm that your role as a planner belongs to the planning layer and not to real-time operational management.
5. Use this work rule as a reference: if you are preparing the future plan, you are likely working in the correct place as a planner.

When you finish this section, you should understand that planning feeds subsequent modules and that your work occurs before tactical execution.

## Differentiating Planning from Operations and Vehicles

After understanding where planning fits within the platform, the next step is to understand what falls within your scope and what does not.

As a planner, you are responsible for preparing the planning logic and ensuring that the scenario is sufficiently coherent for your later use. This means that your work will include tasks such as creating scenarios, validating the planning base, and preparing outputs that can later be consumed by Scheduling, Rostering, and Operations.

Before continuing, keep in mind this idea:
1. Visibility is not the same as responsibility.
2. Although you may see a broad part of the system, you should not intervene in all domains.
3. Your role should remain focused on future planning, not on dispatching or live incident correction.

To distinguish your role from the work of other profiles:
1. Review what you should do as a planner.
   1. Prepare planning scenarios.
   2. Build a coherent base for Scheduling.
   3. Review whether the planning logic makes sense.
   4. Deliver outputs that other modules can use later.
2. Review what **does not** belong to your role.
   1. Resolve same-day incidents in real-time.
   2. Reassign drivers to cover immediate issues.
   3. Act as if you are responsible for live dispatch.
3. Compare your role with **Operations**.
   1. Planning prepares what should occur.
   2. Operations manages what is occurring right now.
4. Compare your role with **Vehicles**.
   1. Planning works with vehicle type logic and future viability.
   2. Vehicles works with the physical fleet, maintenance, and current availability.
5. Use this decision rule when you have doubts:
   1. If you are designing or adjusting the future plan, it belongs to planning.
   2. If you are resolving a live operational issue, it belongs to operations.
   3. If you are updating the physical state or fleet availability, it belongs to vehicles.

When you finish this section, you should be able to explain the difference between planning, operations, and vehicles without mixing responsibilities.

## Confirming What Subsequent Teams Receive from Planning

Your work as a planner does not end when you create a scenario. It ends when you prepare an output that other teams and other modules can actually use.

This is important because a weak planning base generates downstream problems. Rostering may have difficulties organizing the work, operations may be forced to correct more things in real-time, and fleet-related decisions may become harder to interpret.

Before moving to the next quick start, make sure you understand that the outputs of planning should be:
1. Clear.
2. Coherent.
3. Usable.
4. Traceable.

To confirm what the rest of the platform receives from planning:
1. Review the scenario or planning output you are preparing.
2. Ask yourself if another team could understand and use it without having to guess what you meant.
3. Confirm that you are not creating something just for yourself, but a base that other modules will consume later.
4. Check that you are not mixing future planning decisions with today's operational actions.
5. Use this final rule as a reference:
   1. If you are preparing the future plan, you are in the domain of planning.
   2. If you are resolving today's incident, you are in the domain of operations.
   3. If you are correcting the physical availability of the fleet, you are in the domain of vehicles.

When you finish this section, you should be ready to start building the planning foundation in the next quick start.
