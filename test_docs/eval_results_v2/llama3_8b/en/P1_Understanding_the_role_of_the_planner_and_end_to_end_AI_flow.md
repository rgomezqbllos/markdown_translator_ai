## Understanding the Role of the Planner within the Platform

Before creating scenarios or initiating calculations, you need to understand where planning fits within the platform and what is expected of your role.

As a planner, you work on the part of the process that prepares the future operation. Your work does not consist of resolving real-time incidents nor managing the tactical execution of the current day. Your work consists of preparing a planning base that other modules and teams can use later.

Use this quick start when starting for the first time as a planner, when needing to clarify responsibilities between planning and operation, or when wanting to understand what your work should deliver to the rest of the platform.

Before starting, ensure that:
1. You have access to the environment with the role of **Planejador** or with equivalent visibility.
2. You have a basic idea of how the platform is divided into various functional areas.
3. You are willing to understand the flow first, before configuring data or calculating scenarios.

To understand the role of planning within the platform:
1. Open the platform and identify the main areas related to planning, such as trip planning and daily operations.
2. Review the general flow and understand that planning occurs before the execution of daily operations.
3. Think of the process in this order:
   1. **Planning** prepares the future scenario and builds a coherent base.
   2. **Rostering** connects that plan with real people, labor rules, and availability.
   3. **Daily Operations** manages what actually occurs during the current day.
4. Confirm that your role as a planner belongs to the planning layer and not to real-time operational management.
5. Use this work rule as a reference: if you are preparing the future plan, you are probably working in the correct place as a planner.

When you finish this section, you should understand that planning feeds into subsequent modules and that your work occurs before tactical execution.

## Distinguishing Planning from Operations and Vehicles

After understanding where planning fits within the platform, the next step is to understand what belongs to you and what does not.

As a planner, you are responsible for preparing the planning logic and ensuring that the scenario is sufficiently coherent for later use. This means that your work will include tasks such as creating scenarios, validating the planning base, and preparing outputs that can later be consumed by Scheduling, Rostering, and Operations.

Before continuing, keep in mind this idea:
1. Visibility is not the same as responsibility.
2. Although you may see a wide part of the system, you should not intervene in all domains.
3. Your role should remain focused on future planning, not on dispatching or correcting live incidents.

To distinguish your role from the work of other profiles:
1. Review what you should do as a planner.
   1. Prepare planning scenarios.
   2. Build a coherent base for Scheduling.
   3. Review if the plan's logic makes sense.
   4. Deliver outputs that other modules can use later.
2. Review what **does not** belong to your role.
   1. Resolve same-day incidents in real-time.
   2. Reassign drivers to cover immediate problems.
   3. Act as if you were responsible for live dispatching.
3. Compare your role with **Operations**.
   1. Planning prepares what should occur.
   2. Operations manage what is happening right now.
4. Compare your role with **Vehicles**.
   1. Planning works with vehicle type logic and future viability.
   2. Vehicles work with the physical fleet, maintenance, and current availability.
5. Use this decision rule when you are unsure:
   1. If you are designing or adjusting the future plan, it belongs to planning.
   2. If you are resolving a real-time operational problem, it belongs to operations.
   3. If you are updating the physical state or availability of the fleet, it belongs to vehicles.

When you complete this section, you should be able to explain the difference between planning, operations, and vehicles without mixing responsibilities.

## Confirming What Subsequent Teams Receive from Planning

Your work as a planner does not end when you create a scenario. It ends when you prepare an output that other teams and modules can actually use.

This is important because a weak planning base generates problems downstream. Rostering may have difficulties organizing work, operations may be forced to correct more things in real-time, and fleet-related decisions may become more difficult to interpret.

Before moving on to the next quick start, ensure you understand that planning outputs should be:
1. Clear.
2. Coherent.
3. Usable.
4. Trackable.

To confirm what the rest of the platform receives from planning:
1. Review the scenario or planning output you are preparing.
2. Ask yourself if another team could understand and use it without having to guess what you meant to say.
3. Confirm that you are not creating something only for yourself, but a base that other modules will consume later.
4. Check that you are not mixing future planning decisions with operational actions for the same day.
5. Use this final rule as a reference:
   1. If you are preparing the future plan, you are in the domain of planning.
   2. If you are resolving an incident today, you are already in the domain of operations.
   3. If you are correcting physical fleet availability, you are in the domain of vehicles.

When you complete this section, you should be ready to start building the planning foundation in the next quick start.
